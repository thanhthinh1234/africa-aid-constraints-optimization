RE: Aiding Africa: Disaster/Diseaf Relief Plan

Africa is often considered the most disease and disaster-prone continent. African communities suffer from a lack of infrastructure, unstable government, and inadequate resources to cope with natural and health related disasters. Assembling a plan of action to aid Africa can be a challenging but rewarding endeavor. With the help of Tadateru Konoe, the president of the Board of Governors for the IFRC, we have found that all supplies can be sent from two American cities: New York, New York and Jacksonville, Florida. Our goal is to reach three strategic cities in Africa: Niamey, Niger; Kosongo, D.R. of the Congo; and N’djamena, Chad. Below we have outlined three separate plans of actions along with visualizations regarding minimizing costs, minimizing time of travel, and maximizing aid to each city:

For the first plan, we found how quickly we can move workers between each pair of cities without considering limitations on the number of workers and amount of cargo that can be transferred between any cities. Shown in the figure above, we can cover six of our nine cities within the range of 24 hours via airplanes (Khartoum - 17.6 hours; Lusaka - 19.86 hours; and Nairobi - 19.9 hours) to 8 days via ships (Dakar - 4.7 days; Libreville - 7.2 days; and Luanda - 7.8 days). We expect to reach our final cities within a few hours each by flight (Kosongo – 1.87 hours; N’djamena - 3 hours; and Niamey - 5.16 hours). Routes that go through Khartoum, Sudan can create a significant time bottleneck because it acts as an intermediary for the most destinations.

For the second plan, we recognized the cost of workers and sending supplies for each trip and solved for the number of trips on each path while minimizing costs. There were some transportation constraints on the number of airplanes and trucks we could use. We can cover all six of our first leg cities by 3600 flights and 1917 ships with an approximate cost of $254,000,000. While trucks are not used directly from the US due to their limited capacity, they will become our main means of transportation to help deliver aid to reach our final destinations with 10169 trips, along with 1200 flights costing $54,816,000. Potential bottlenecks arise in Jacksonville and New York; because we are minimizing costs, it would be beneficial if we could somehow use less supplies to aid the cities.

For the third and final plan, we added more restrictions on travel that were found by Tadateru Konoe. These restrictions prevent the IFRC from reaching minimum aid in each city, so we solved to maximize the amount of cargo that can be sent to each. Three cities struggling with these restrictions and not meeting the aid requirements are Lusaka, Zambia (120,000/150,000 or 80%), Konsogo, D.R. Congo (40,125/180,000 or 22%) and N’djamena, Chad (66,046/80,000 or 83%). Despite all these restrictions mentioned above, we are still able to send a total of 816,170 tons to all nine cities, which is far from the 1 million needed. The reasons for this lack of aid, and the potential bottlenecks are located on routes that go to both Kosongo and to N’djamena.

Through the analysis of the three plans, we have narrowed down more and more what the necessary requirements and what the appropriate outcomes of our project will be. In the first plan we found that the shortest route is between Khartoum and N’djamena approximating three hours. Second, we found that it will require at least $310,861,300 to meet the needs of all nine IFRC cities. Lastly, after adding in additional restrictions on trucks and airplanes, we found that we can get at most 816,170 tons to all nine cities with problems on routes to Kosongo, N’djamena, and Lusaka, leaving them with less than the required aid. Since Tadateru Konoe has already tried his best to find other routes, it is likely that we will have to send less aid than is required if we cannot find a way to negotiate looser restrictions on routes to each of those cities.
